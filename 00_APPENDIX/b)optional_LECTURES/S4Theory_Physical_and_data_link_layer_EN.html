The Cable That Silenced an Ocean

16 August 1858 — and a problem we still face today

A Real Event

On 16 August 1858, Queen Victoria sent the first transatlantic telegraph message to US President James Buchanan. The 98‑word message took 16 hours to transmit — a journey that previously required weeks by ship.

Within three weeks, the cable had failed. The cause: signal attenuation over 3 200 km of copper, compounded by excessive voltage that destroyed the insulation. A reliable replacement took eight more years.

The challenges of 1858 — attenuation, noise and synchronisation — are precisely the challenges we still solve at the physical layer.

🎯 Today's Question

Every WhatsApp message, every database query, every API call must ultimately become electrical signals on a wire or radio waves in the air.

But raw signals have no addresses, no error detection, no structure. How do we go from a stream of voltage changes to an organised, addressable frame?

We shall return to this

By slide 36, you will be able to explain exactly what went wrong with that 1858 cable — and what modern physical-layer engineering does differently.

The Problem: Raw Bits Are Not Enough

What goes wrong when all you have is a stream of voltage changes?

🚫 What Layer 1 alone cannot do

  • No addressing — a bit stream has no "from" or "to"
  • No error detection — a flipped bit passes through silently
  • No framing — where does one message end and the next begin?
  • No access control — what if two devices transmit simultaneously?

✓ What we need Layer 2 to provide

  • Structured units (frames) with clear boundaries
  • Source and destination addresses (MAC)
  • Error detection (CRC checksum)
  • Medium access rules (who transmits when)

An analogy

Imagine shouting across a crowded room with no names, no language and no turn‑taking rules. Layer 1 gives you the ability to make sound. Layer 2 gives you words, names and the social convention of waiting your turn to speak.

What If There Were No Frames?

A thought experiment before we begin

🤔 Scenario

Two computers, A and B, are connected by a single copper wire. Computer A wants to send the message HELLO (5 bytes = 40 bits) followed immediately by BYE (3 bytes = 24 bits). Computer B sees a continuous stream of 64 bits arrive. How does B know where HELLO ends and BYE begins?

It cannot — not without a framing protocol. This is precisely the problem the data link layer solves. By wrapping each message in a frame with a preamble (start delimiter), length/type field and a trailing checksum (FCS), the receiver can unambiguously determine message boundaries, verify integrity and route the payload to the correct upper‑layer protocol.

This is Layer 2's most fundamental contribution: turning a shapeless bit stream into discrete, addressable, verifiable units of data.

I

The Physical Layer

Signals, media, coding and modulation — how bits travel through the physical world

What Does the Physical Layer Do?

Layer 1 in the OSI model

Definition

The physical layer handles the transmission of raw, unstructured bits over a physical medium. It defines the electrical, optical or radio signals used to represent binary data, along with parameters such as data rate, synchronisation, connector types and maximum cable distances.

⚡ Signal Type

Electrical (copper), optical (fibre) or electromagnetic (radio). The choice of signal determines the medium, the encoding scheme and the achievable distance.

⏱️ Timing

Bit duration, clock synchronisation and baud rate. The sender and receiver must agree on when each bit starts and ends.

🔌 Physical Specs

Connector pinouts (RJ‑45, LC, SC), cable categories (Cat5e, Cat6a), voltage levels and maximum segment lengths.

Transmission Media

Guided vs unguided — how signals travel

🔌 Guided (wired)

Copper: coaxial cable (historical, still used for cable TV), twisted pair (UTP/STP — the dominant LAN medium). Signals are electrical.

Optical fibre: single‑mode (SMF, long distance, laser) and multi‑mode (MMF, short distance, LED). Signals are light pulses. Immune to electromagnetic interference (EMI).

📡 Unguided (wireless)

Radio: Wi‑Fi (802.11), Bluetooth, LTE/5G. Signals propagate through the air. Subject to interference, multipath fading and shared‑medium contention.

Properties That Limit a Medium

Why signals degrade — and why it matters

📉 Attenuation

Signal amplitude decreases with distance. Energy is lost to resistance (copper) or absorption (fibre). Beyond a certain distance, the signal becomes indistinguishable from noise.

⚡ Noise & Interference

External electromagnetic sources (motors, fluorescent lights, adjacent cables) corrupt the signal. Crosstalk in twisted pair occurs when signals in one pair induce current in a neighbouring pair.

📊 Bandwidth vs Bitrate

Bandwidth (Hz) is the range of frequencies a medium supports. Bitrate (bit/s) is the number of bits transmitted per second. They are related — wider bandwidth enables higher bitrate — but they are not the same quantity.

Common Misconception

"Fibre is always faster than copper." Not necessarily. Both can achieve 10+ Gbit/s over short distances. Fibre's real advantages are distance (tens of kilometres without repeaters) and EMI immunity. For a 5‑metre patch in a server rack, Cat6a copper is often the more practical choice.

Line Coding: The Synchronisation Problem

Why we cannot simply use "high voltage = 1, low voltage = 0"

The Core Problem

Line coding defines how binary data is represented as voltage levels on a wire. The receiver's clock must stay synchronised with the sender's clock. Long runs of identical bits (e.g., 00000000) produce no voltage transitions — and without transitions, the receiver's clock drifts.

NRZ (Non‑Return‑to‑Zero)

The simplest scheme: 1 = high voltage, 0 = low voltage (or vice versa). No transition occurs when consecutive bits are identical.

Why Is This a Problem?

Consider transmitting 256 consecutive zeros. With NRZ, the voltage stays flat for 256 bit periods. The receiver has no edge to lock its clock onto. Even a tiny frequency mismatch between sender and receiver accumulates — after 256 bits, the receiver may have miscounted by an entire bit position.

This motivates the invention of smarter encoding schemes: NRZI and Manchester.

NRZI and Manchester Encoding

Trading bandwidth for synchronisation

NRZI (NRZ Inverted)

A 1 causes a transition; a 0 keeps the current level. Solves the problem for runs of 1s (every 1 produces an edge) but not for runs of 0s.

Modulation

Encoding data onto a carrier wave for wireless (and some wired) transmission

Why Modulation?

Wireless communication requires a carrier wave — a high‑frequency sinusoid that propagates well through the air. Modulation varies a property of the carrier to encode information. The receiver demodulates to recover the original data.

ASK

Amplitude Shift Keying — varies the carrier's amplitude. Simple but vulnerable to noise.

FSK

Frequency Shift Keying — varies the carrier's frequency. More resilient to noise than ASK.

PSK

Phase Shift Keying — varies the carrier's phase. Widely used in Wi‑Fi and digital communications.

QAM — Quadrature Amplitude Modulation

Combines amplitude and phase changes to encode multiple bits per symbol. 16‑QAM encodes 4 bits/symbol; 256‑QAM encodes 8 bits/symbol. Used in modern Wi‑Fi (802.11ac/ax) and cable broadband (DOCSIS). Higher QAM orders yield more throughput but demand better signal‑to‑noise ratios.

The Bandwidth Trade‑off

Every engineering decision at the physical layer involves trade‑offs. Manchester gives perfect synchronisation at the cost of doubling the required signal rate (baud rate). Modern schemes like 4B/5B and 8B/10B achieve a better balance: they limit long runs of identical bits with only 25% overhead instead of 100%.

II

The Data Link Layer

Frames, addresses, error detection and medium access — organising the bit stream

From Signal to Frame

The transition from Layer 1 to Layer 2

Layer 1 delivers…

A raw, unstructured bit stream. It faithfully converts between bits and signals but has no concept of messages, addresses or integrity checks.

Layer 2 provides…

Structured frames with clear boundaries (delimitation), source and destination addresses (MAC), error detection (CRC) and medium access services to upper layers.

The relationship

Layer 2 is to Layer 1 what grammar is to pronunciation. Layer 1 ensures sounds (bits) are produced correctly. Layer 2 organises those sounds into words (frames) with meaning (addresses, checksums) and turn‑taking rules (access control).

Data Link Layer Structure

Two sublayers with distinct responsibilities

LLC (IEEE 802.2)

  • Interface between software and the data link layer
  • Independent of the physical medium
  • Flow control (where applicable)
  • Multiplexing for upper‑layer protocols

MAC

  • Builds the actual frames
  • Handles source/destination addressing (MAC addresses)
  • Manages medium access control (who transmits when)
  • Technology‑dependent: Ethernet MAC ≠ Wi‑Fi MAC

MAC Sublayer Functions

What the MAC sublayer does for every frame

🔲 Frame Delimitation

Marks the start and end of each frame using a preamble and start‑of‑frame delimiter. This solves the "where does HELLO end and BYE begin?" problem from slide 7.

🏷️ Addressing

Attaches source and destination MAC addresses (48 bits each) so the frame can be directed to the correct recipient on the local network.

🔍 Error Detection

Appends a CRC (Cyclic Redundancy Check) — also called FCS (Frame Check Sequence) — computed over the frame contents. The receiver recomputes and compares; a mismatch means the frame is corrupted.

🚦 Medium Access Control

Determines when a station may transmit. On shared media (legacy Ethernet hubs, Wi‑Fi), multiple devices contend for the same channel. The MAC sublayer implements algorithms — CSMA/CD for Ethernet, CSMA/CA for Wi‑Fi — to manage this contention and minimise collisions.

Layer 2 Encapsulation

Wrapping data into a frame — the general pattern

Every Layer 2 technology uses the same general idea: wrap the payload from the layer above in a header (front) and a trailer (back). The specific field sizes and names vary by technology, but the structure is consistent.

Encapsulation is recursive

The "payload" field of a Layer 2 frame contains a Layer 3 packet, which itself contains a Layer 4 segment, which contains application data. Each layer wraps the one above it — like nested envelopes. This is the essence of protocol layering.

Ethernet (IEEE 802.3)

The dominant Layer 2 technology — from 10 Mbit/s to 400 Gbit/s

Definition

Ethernet is a family of wired LAN technologies standardised as IEEE 802.3. First developed by Robert Metcalfe at Xerox PARC in 1973, it has evolved from 10 Mbit/s coaxial bus networks to modern 400 Gbit/s switched fibre links, while retaining a remarkably stable frame format.

Preamble + SFD

7 bytes of alternating 1s and 0s (10101010) followed by the Start Frame Delimiter (10101011). This allows the receiver to synchronise its clock before the actual frame data begins.

Type / EtherType

Identifies the upper‑layer protocol: 0x0800 = IPv4, 0x0806 = ARP, 0x86DD = IPv6. Values ≤ 1500 indicate a length field (IEEE 802.3 original).

MAC Addresses (48 Bits)

The hardware identity of every network interface

Structure

A MAC address is 48 bits (6 bytes), written in hexadecimal as AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF. The first 24 bits form the OUI (Organizationally Unique Identifier), assigned by IEEE to the manufacturer. The last 24 bits are the interface identifier, assigned by the manufacturer to each NIC.

🏷️ Special Addresses

  • FF:FF:FF:FF:FF:FF — broadcast (all stations on the LAN)
  • Bit 0 of the first octet: 0 = unicast, 1 = multicast
  • Bit 1 of the first octet: 0 = globally unique (OUI), 1 = locally administered

Ethernet Collisions and CSMA/CD

How Ethernet handles simultaneous transmissions on shared media

CSMA/CD — Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Detection

1) Listen before transmitting (carrier sense). 2) Transmit if the medium is idle. 3) Detect a collision if two stations transmit simultaneously — both see garbled signals. 4) Abort transmission, send a jam signal, then 5) wait a random back‑off time before retrying (binary exponential back‑off).

⚠ Historical Context

CSMA/CD was essential when Ethernet ran on shared coaxial bus networks (10BASE‑2, 10BASE‑5) and hubs. All stations shared a single collision domain.

✓ Modern Reality

Modern Ethernet uses switches with full‑duplex links. Each port is its own collision domain. In practice, collisions have been eliminated on switched Ethernet. CSMA/CD remains in the standard but is rarely exercised.

No. Full‑duplex means the station can send and receive simultaneously on separate wire pairs. There is no shared medium to contend for, and CSMA/CD is disabled. Collisions on modern switched networks indicate a hardware fault or a misconfigured half‑duplex link.

Other Layer 2 Issues (Ethernet)

Problems beyond collisions

🌀 Switching Loops

If redundant links create a cycle between switches, broadcast frames circulate endlessly — a broadcast storm. Unlike IP packets (which have a TTL), Ethernet frames have no hop limit. The Spanning Tree Protocol (STP, IEEE 802.1D) prevents this by disabling redundant links.

📏 Runt Frames

Frames shorter than the 64‑byte minimum (preamble excluded). Usually caused by a collision that interrupted transmission before the full frame was sent. Switches silently discard them.

📏 Jabber Frames

Frames exceeding the 1518‑byte maximum (or 1522 with 802.1Q VLAN tag). Typically caused by a faulty NIC that fails to stop transmitting. Also discarded by the switch.

Wi‑Fi (IEEE 802.11)

Wireless LAN — same layer, different rules

📡 The Medium: Air

Wi‑Fi uses radio waves in the 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz bands (and 6 GHz for Wi‑Fi 6E). The medium is inherently shared — every station within range can hear every transmission.

Channels may overlap (2.4 GHz has only 3 non‑overlapping channels: 1, 6 and 11 in most regions), causing co‑channel interference.

📋 Frame Types

  • Management: beacons, probe requests/responses, authentication, association
  • Control: RTS, CTS, ACK
  • Data: the actual payload frames

A Wi‑Fi frame can carry up to 4 MAC addresses (source, destination, transmitter and receiver) depending on whether the frame crosses an AP.

Wi‑Fi Collisions and CSMA/CA

Why wireless cannot detect collisions — and what it does instead

CSMA/CA — Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Avoidance

1) Listen (carrier sense). 2) If idle, wait a random back‑off period (unlike Ethernet, which transmits immediately). 3) Transmit. 4) Wait for an ACK from the receiver. If no ACK arrives, assume a collision occurred and retry with an increased back‑off window.

⚠ Why Not Collision Detection?

A wireless station cannot listen while transmitting — its own signal drowns out everything else (the near‑far problem). So Wi‑Fi avoids collisions (by deferring) rather than detecting them (as Ethernet does).

✓ Optional: RTS/CTS

To address the hidden node problem (A and C can both reach B but not each other), Wi‑Fi supports an RTS/CTS handshake that reserves the channel before data transmission.

Wi‑Fi Interface Modes and Authentication

How a wireless adapter operates — and how access is controlled

📱 Interface Modes

  • Managed (client): connects to an access point
  • AP (access point): accepts client connections
  • AP‑VLAN: access point with VLAN tagging
  • P2P: Wi‑Fi Direct, device‑to‑device
  • Monitor: passive Layer 2 sniffing — captures all frames including management and control. Used for debugging and security auditing.

🔐 Authentication Standards

  • WEP — obsolete, trivially broken (RC4 key reuse)
  • WPA — obsolete, TKIP has known weaknesses
  • WPA2 — widely deployed, AES‑CCMP
  • WPA3 — current standard, SAE handshake replaces PSK, forward secrecy

III

Switching and Segmentation

How switches learn, forward and segment the network

Repeater, Bridge and Switch

Three generations of Layer 1/Layer 2 devices

Repeater / Hub

Layer 1 device. Receives a signal on one port, amplifies it and floods it out all other ports. No intelligence — every port is in the same collision domain.

Bridge

Layer 2 device with a small number of ports (typically 2–4). Reads MAC addresses and makes simple forward/filter decisions. Separates collision domains but not broadcast domains.

Switch

A multi‑port bridge with hardware‑accelerated forwarding. Each port is its own collision domain. Uses a CAM table (Content Addressable Memory) to forward frames only to the correct port.

Common Misconception

"A switch and a hub are basically the same thing." They are not. A hub is a Layer 1 repeater — it copies every frame to every port. A switch learns which MAC address is behind which port and forwards selectively. This difference is fundamental to network performance and security.

CAM Table: Learning, Forwarding and Ageing

How a switch builds its forwarding intelligence

📖 Learning

When a frame arrives on port N, the switch records the source MAC → port N mapping in its CAM table.

📤 Forwarding / Flooding

If the destination MAC is in the table, forward to that port only. If unknown, flood the frame out all ports except the source port.

⏱ Ageing

Entries expire after a timeout (typically 300 s). This ensures that if a host moves to a different port, stale entries are removed.

VLANs — Virtual Local Area Networks

Logical segmentation on a single physical switch

Definition

A VLAN divides a physical switch into multiple logical broadcast domains. Frames in VLAN 10 are invisible to ports in VLAN 20, even though they share the same hardware. Inter‑VLAN communication requires a Layer 3 router.

🏷️ IEEE 802.1Q Tagging

On trunk links (switch‑to‑switch or switch‑to‑router), an extra 4‑byte tag is inserted into the Ethernet frame after the source MAC. It contains a 12‑bit VLAN ID (values 0–4095), allowing up to 4094 VLANs on a single trunk.

🎯 Why VLANs?

  • Security: isolate sensitive traffic (finance, HR, guest)
  • Performance: smaller broadcast domains = less broadcast noise
  • Flexibility: group by function, not by physical location

Returning to 1858: What Went Wrong?

Closing the narrative — the transatlantic cable revisited

The Answer

The 1858 transatlantic cable failed for reasons we can now name precisely. Attenuation over 3 200 km of copper reduced the signal to near‑noise levels. The chief electrician, Wildman Whitehouse, compensated by increasing the transmission voltage to 2 000 V — which destroyed the gutta‑percha insulation (medium failure). There was no error detection, no encoding to ensure synchronisation and no way to frame discrete messages reliably.

The 1866 replacement succeeded because William Thomson (Lord Kelvin) insisted on lower voltage, a more sensitive receiver and a mirror galvanometer that worked with the weak signal rather than trying to overpower attenuation with brute force.

The modern lesson

Every concept from today's lecture addresses one of those 1858 failures: line coding (synchronisation), CRC (error detection), frame structure (message boundaries), repeaters and amplifiers (attenuation). The problems have not changed — only the solutions have become more sophisticated.

  • ARP resolution fails (host unreachable on same subnet)
  • Broadcast storms (CPU spikes on all switches)

From Layer 2 to Layer 3

MAC addresses are flat — IP addresses are hierarchical

MAC — Flat Addressing

A MAC address (00:1A:2B:3C:4D:5E) identifies a specific interface globally. It encodes the manufacturer (OUI) but nothing about location. A switch cannot route traffic based on MAC addresses beyond the local segment.

IP — Hierarchical Addressing

An IP address (192.168.1.100) encodes both a network and a host component. This hierarchy enables routing: a router examines the network prefix and forwards the packet toward the correct network, without needing to know every individual host.

The bridge between L2 and L3: ARP

When a host knows the destination IP but not the MAC, it broadcasts an ARP (Address Resolution Protocol) request: "Who has 192.168.1.1? Tell 192.168.1.100." The owner replies with its MAC address. This mapping is cached in the ARP table (we shall explore this in the seminar).

Final Question: Synthesis

Putting it all together — a question that requires knowledge from the full lecture

A host on VLAN 10 sends a frame to a host on VLAN 20 connected to the same physical switch. What happens?

A) The switch forwards the frame directly because both hosts are on the same hardware
B) The frame is broadcast to all ports on the switch
C) The switch floods the frame only to ports in VLAN 10 (not VLAN 20)
D) The frame reaches VLAN 20 because it is a Layer 3 router

Why this is the key insight

VLANs create separate broadcast domains. Even if the destination host is physically one metre away on the same switch, the frame will never leave VLAN 10 without a Layer 3 device (router or L3 switch) to route between VLANs. The switch floods only within the VLAN. This is why VLANs provide genuine traffic isolation — not merely administrative convenience.
